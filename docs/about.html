Who I Am

I’m a PhD researcher in Psychology at the University of Hull, funded by the White Rose Doctoral Training Partnership (WRDTP). My work sits at the fascinating—and sometimes unsettling—intersection of human cognition and artificial intelligence. I spend my days exploring how our minds adapt, resist, and evolve as AI-generated content becomes increasingly indistinguishable from reality.

My research focuses on AI-generated imagery and misinformation: How do we cognitively process AI-images differently than “real” photographs? What happens when the line between authentic and fabricated blurs? These questions drive my PhD project, but my curiosity extends far beyond that—into the effects of AI-generated pornography, philosophical debates about what counts as “real” or “fake” information, and the broader implications of living in an AI-saturated world. (You can explore more of my thoughts on these topics in my blog!)

My Journey

Before landing at Hull for my PhD, I completed a fully-funded Master’s of Social Research (MSc) at the same institution. My path into research began earlier, though, at Nanyang Technological University in Singapore, where I earned my Bachelor’s degree in Psychology. During my time at NTU, I was honored as an NTU President Research Scholar for two consecutive years—a recognition tied to my work in the Undergraduate Research Experience on Campus (URECA) programme.

Through URECA, I had the opportunity to lead two independent, year-long research projects under the guidance of faculty mentors. Those experiences solidified my love for research—the thrill of asking questions no one has answered yet, the satisfaction of uncovering patterns in messy data, and the joy of contributing something new to our understanding of human behavior.
